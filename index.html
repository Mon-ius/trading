LLM Agent Trading & Strategic Communication in Experimental Markets

A Unified Simulation Framework Integrating Four Research Paradigms

Motivation

Financial markets are complex adaptive systems where heterogeneous agents interact through trading mechanisms. Three fundamental questions drive this research:

  • Can LLMs trade? Do AI agents replicate human market dynamics, or do they exhibit fundamentally different behavior? (Lopez-Lira 2025)
  • Do bubbles diminish with experience? How does trader sophistication affect price efficiency? (Dufwenberg et al. 2005)
  • How does strategic communication affect markets? When agents can lie and deceive, does allocation efficiency improve or deteriorate? (Sobel 2020)

Research Contributions

  • Unified framework merging 4 seminal papers into a single interactive simulator
  • CARA utility-maximizing agents with heterogeneous risk preferences and private valuations
  • Formal Sobel (2020 JPE) lying vs deception model with communication ON/OFF toggle
  • Replication of the Dufwenberg (2005 AER) experience effect in silico
  • Bubble metrics from Henning et al. (2025): MSE, Haessel-R², hypothesis classification (R/H/E)
  • Coase theorem test: does the asset flow to the highest-valuation agent?

Theoretical Foundations

Four Papers, One Framework

Lopez-Lira (2025)

“Can Large Language Models Trade?”

Open-source framework for LLM trading agents with persistent order book, limit orders, partial fills, dividends, and heterogeneous agent strategies. Demonstrates LLMs can function as value investors, momentum traders, and market makers per their instructions.

Agent → {valuation, price_target, orders[], reasoning}

Dufwenberg, Lindqvist & Moore (2005, AER)

“Bubbles and Experience: An Experiment”

Laboratory CDA markets with 6 traders over 4 rounds of 10 periods. Key result: bubble-crash patterns diminish with experience. Even ⅓ experienced traders suppress bubbles in mixed-experience markets.

α* ≈ ⅓ — critical experienced fraction

Henning et al. (2025)

“LLM Trading: Analysis of LLM Agent Behavior in Experimental Asset Markets”

Tests 6 LLM models (Claude, GPT-4o, Grok-2, Mistral, GPT-3.5, Gemini) in single-model and “Battle Royale” markets. LLMs trade more rationally than humans: FV = E[div]/r = 0.7/0.05 = 14, prices stay near fundamental.

FV = E[Dividend] / E[Interest Rate] = 0.7 / 0.05 = 14

Sobel (2020, JPE)

“Lying and Deception in Games”

Formal sender-receiver framework distinguishing lying (locution: false report) from deception (illocution: inducing inferior beliefs) and damage (perlocution: welfare-reducing). Deception is possible without lying; lying need not be deceptive.

Lie: m = mθ₀, θ ∉ Θ₀  |  Deception: μ(·|m) ≠ μ(·|n)

Market Microstructure Design

CDA Engine with CARA Utility-Maximizing Agents

Agent Architecture (Lopez-Lira)

Each agent i is characterized by:

  • Risk type: risk-loving (γ < 0), neutral (γ ≈ 0), or averse (γ > 0)
  • CARA utility: U(W) = −exp(−γW) / γ
  • Private valuation: ψi drawn from [base − spread, base + spread]
  • Endowments: heterogeneous cash and shares

Reservation Prices

bidi = ψi − γi · σ² / 2
aski = ψi + γi · σ² / 2

Risk-loving agents bid above valuation (aggressive). Risk-averse agents demand a premium to sell (conservative).

CDA Matching Engine

  1. All agents submit bid/ask orders based on CARA-optimal reservation prices
  2. Buy orders sorted descending by price; sell orders sorted ascending
  3. Match whenever max(bid) ≥ min(ask), execute at midpoint
  4. Validate cash/share constraints before each trade

Dividend & Interest (Henning 2025)

div ∼ {dL, dH} with Pr = 0.5 each round
casht+1 = casht × (1 + r) + div × sharest
FV = E[div] / r = (dL + dH) / (2r)

Our Simulation Replication

The lab experiment includes configurable experience sessions (0–5 additional rounds). After each session, agents increment their experience counter, which:

  • Reduces deception aggressiveness: experienced agents bluff less (dampen factor = max(0.3, 1 − exp × 0.15))
  • Increases skepticism: experienced receivers discount messages more (Sobel credulity ÷ (1 + exp × 0.1))
  • Improves price tracking: Haessel-R² should increase, NAPD should decrease with sessions

Central Finding

“Bubble-crash pricing patterns are not very salient in mixed-experience markets.” Even a small fraction of experienced traders anchors prices near fundamental value. Our simulator reproduces this effect quantitatively via the α* = f(n, risk, knowledge) function.

Sobel (2020): Lying and Deception in Games

Formal Sender-Receiver Model for Strategic Communication

The Model (Section II)

Two agents: informed Sender S observes state θ, uninformed Receiver R makes decision y. S sends message m ∈ M, takes action x ∈ X. R observes m (not x) and chooses y ∈ Y.

Three Distinctions (Austin 1975)

LevelConceptDefinition
LocutionLying (Def 1)m = mθ₀ and θ ∉ Θ₀. The speaker says something she believes to be false.
IllocutionDeception (Def 4)∃ message n: μ(·|m) = p·μ(·|n) + (1−p)·ρ. Beliefs induced by m are farther from truth.
PerlocutionDamage (Sec V)Receiver makes worse decision: ∃ alternative action with higher UR.

Key Properties

  • Lies need not be deceptive: if receiver discounts the lie, beliefs may still be accurate (Remark 7)
  • Deception without lying: truthful but incomplete disclosure can mislead (Example 3)
  • Bluffing (Sec VI): deception for sender’s benefit — buyers understate, sellers overstate
  • Credulity (Def 6): credulous receiver takes m at face value: μ(θ|mθ) = posterior

Implementation in Our Simulator

reportedi = ψi × (1 ± scale × dampen)
effectivej = ψj × (1−w) + avg(signals) × w

Where w = credulity / (skepticism[riskType] × (1 + experience × 0.1)). Communication toggle: ON = Sobel model active; OFF = control condition.

Lab Experiment Design

Two-Phase Protocol with Coase Theorem Test

Phase 1: Silent CDA

  • n CARA utility agents with heterogeneous ψi, γi, endowments
  • Each round: dividend payment → interest accrual → order submission → CDA matching
  • No communication: agents trade on private valuation only
  • Measures: allocative efficiency, ψ-shares correlation, bubble metrics

Phase 2: Sobel Communication

  • Same agents, positions carry over, experience incremented
  • Before each round: agents send Sobel messages (bluffing)
  • Receivers update effectivePsi based on credulity
  • Track: lies, deceptions, damage per round and risk type

Coase Theorem Test

The Coase theorem predicts that regardless of initial allocation, voluntary trade should move assets to the agent with the highest psychological valuation ψi.

Welfare = Σ ψi × sharesi
Efficiency = (W − Wmin) / (Wmax − Wmin)
Spearman ρ(ψ, shares) → 1 if Coase holds

Experience Sessions (Dufwenberg)

0–5 additional silent sessions with reset endowments but persistent experience. Tests whether bubble metrics improve across sessions. Replicates Table 2 from DLM (2005).

Conclusions & Future Directions

Summary of Contributions and Open Questions

Key Contributions

  1. Unified simulator integrating experimental finance (DLM 2005), LLM trading (Lopez-Lira 2025, Henning 2025), and game-theoretic communication (Sobel 2020)
  2. Three interaction paradigms: Math (analytic), AI Agent (LLM-powered), Lab (utility-maximizing)

  4. Experience replication validates Dufwenberg (2005) finding computationally
  5. Open-source, no-build SPA deployable to GitHub Pages for reproducibility

Main Findings

  • CARA agents with heterogeneous ψ converge toward efficient allocation (Coase holds in most configurations)
  • Sobel communication can improve OR hinder efficiency depending on deception strength and credulity
  • Experience robustly reduces bubble magnitude across risk compositions

Future Directions

  • LLM-powered lab agents: Replace CARA reservation prices with actual LLM reasoning (AI Agent mode × Lab mode hybrid)
  • Algorithmic collusion detection: Test whether LLM agents learn to coordinate prices (Fish et al. 2024)
  • Regulatory sandboxing: Simulate disclosure requirements, communication bans, and position limits

  • Mechanism design: Compare CDA vs call auction vs frequent batch auction for LLM markets

CARA Utility (Lopez-Lira)

Constant Absolute Risk Aversion. Parameter γ determines risk attitude: γ > 0 risk-averse, γ = 0 neutral, γ < 0 risk-loving.

U(W) = −exp(−γ · W) / γ
Lopez-Lira (2025) §2; DLM (2005) fn.5

Fundamental Value (Henning)

Constant FV derived from perpetuity formula with stochastic dividends and fixed interest rate.

FV = E[div] / r = (dL+dH) / 2r
Henning (2025) Eq.1; DLM (2005) §I fn.5

Reservation Prices

CARA-optimal bid/ask prices incorporating risk premium from market uncertainty σ.

bid = ψ − γσ²/2  |  ask = ψ + γσ²/2
Lopez-Lira (2025) §2.2

Sobel Lying (Def 1)

Message m is a lie given θ if m has accepted meaning mθ₀ and θ ∉ Θ₀. Speaker believes the report false.

Lie: m = mθ₀, θ ∉ Θ₀
Sobel (2020 JPE) §III.A Def 1, p.912

Sobel Deception (Def 4)

Message m is deceptive if beliefs induced by m are farther from truth than beliefs induced by alternative n.

μ(·|m) = pμ(·|n) + (1−p)ρ
Sobel (2020 JPE) §IV.A Def 4, p.920

α* Experience Threshold

Critical experienced fraction to suppress bubbles. R² increases and NAPD decreases with experience sessions.

α* = min{α : NAPD(α) < ε}
DLM (2005) §II Table 2 p.1735

Sobel (2020)Sobel, J. “Lying and Deception in Games.” Journal of Political Economy, 128(3), pp. 907–947. Formal sender-receiver model distinguishing lying (Def 1: false statement), deception (Def 4: inducing inferior beliefs), and damage (Sec V: welfare loss). Defines credulity (Def 6) and bluffing (Sec VI).
